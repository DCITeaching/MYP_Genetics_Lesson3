🧬 What Is a Mutation?

A mutation is any permanent change in the DNA sequence — a change in the order of nucleotide bases. Mutations can arise from mistakes during DNA replication, exposure to mutagens (radiation, UV light, cigarette smoke), or viral infections. Importantly, not all mutations are harmful — many are neutral or even beneficial, and mutations are the engine of evolution.

The most critical question about any mutation is: where did it occur?

Somatic Cell Mutations Occur in body cells. Passed on by mitosis within that individual. Can cause cancer. Cannot be inherited by offspring — these cells never become eggs or sperm.
Germ Line Mutations Occur in cells that produce gametes. Passed on by meiosis. Can be inherited — every cell in the offspring will carry the mutation. This is how genetic disorders are passed to children.

📊 Types of Mutations

Mutations fall into two main categories — gene mutations that affect a single gene, and chromosome mutations that affect large segments or the number of chromosomes.

Frameshift vs. Point Mutation — Why frameshifts are usually worse:A point mutation (substitution) changes ONE codon — it might change one amino acid, or might be silent. A frameshift shifts the ENTIRE reading frame from the mutation site forward — like removing a letter from "THE CAT SAT" → "TEC ATS AT..." — complete nonsense downstream. Usually produces a non-functional or truncated protein.

🌳 How to Read a Pedigree

A pedigree is a diagram that traces the inheritance of a trait or disease through a family across multiple generations. Doctors and genetic counselors use pedigrees to identify whether a trait is dominant or recessive, autosomal or sex-linked — without sequencing a single gene.

Pedigree Symbol Key
⬜ Unaffected male
⬛ Affected male
⭕ Unaffected female
⚫ Affected female
⬜—⭕ Mating pair
| = offspring line

Step-by-step pattern identification:

Step 1 — Check sex ratioAre significantly more males affected than females? → Likely X-linked. If roughly equal → autosomal.
Step 2 — Find the key familyLook for two UNAFFECTED parents with an AFFECTED child. → Autosomal Recessive (parents are carriers). OR find an AFFECTED parent whose child is UNAFFECTED — could be dominant but not guaranteed dominant to child.
Step 3 — Check for generation skippingDoes the trait skip generations (appears, disappears, reappears)? → Likely recessive. Does it appear in EVERY generation? → Likely dominant.
Step 4 — Check the X-linked clueIf X-linked recessive: do affected FATHERS have affected SONS? No? → That confirms X-linked (sons get Y from dad, not his X).
PatternKey CluesReal Examples
Autosomal RecessiveSkips generations; both sexes equally affected; unaffected parents can have affected child (both are carriers)Cystic fibrosis, Sickle cell, PKU, Tay-Sachs
Autosomal DominantAppears every generation; both sexes equally affected; unaffected individuals NEVER have affected childrenHuntington's disease, Achondroplasia (dwarfism)
X-linked RecessiveMany more males affected; skips generations through carrier females; affected fathers do NOT pass to sonsHemophilia, Color blindness, Duchenne MD

✅ Lesson 3 — Key Takeaways

Somatic vs. Germ LineSomatic mutation = affects only the individual, can cause cancer, NOT inherited. Germ line mutation = can be inherited by ALL cells of the offspring.
Point MutationChanges ONE nucleotide. May be silent, change one amino acid, or create a stop codon. Example: GAG → GUG = sickle cell anemia.
Frameshift MutationInsertion or deletion of nucleotide(s) — shifts reading frame for ALL codons downstream. Usually more damaging than point mutations.
Chromosome MutationsAffect large segments or chromosome number. Nondisjunction (fail to separate) → wrong chromosome number → Down syndrome (Trisomy 21).
Pedigree Reading1) Check sex ratio (X-linked if mostly males). 2) Two unaffected parents → affected child = recessive. 3) Appears every generation = dominant.
X-linked ClueAffected fathers CANNOT pass X-linked trait to sons (sons get Y from dad). This is the most reliable clue for X-linked inheritance.
